A 27-year-old woman, 27 million viewers and a thousand-year-old ceremony - this is why Queen Elizabeth II’s Coronation in 1953 was the most famous royal event in history

Queen Elizabeth II was crowned in 1953 in a ceremony of pomp and splendour. We tell the story of this momentous day in her life

Author:
Laura Silverman
Published Date:
14 April 2026
SHARE THIS ARTICLE

The coronation of Queen Elizabeth II was the most British of days: a day of glory and spectacle, steeped in tradition and uniting the Commonwealth. And it rained.

The day was 2 June 1953, the new Queen Elizabeth II had acceded to the throne on 6 February 1952, when her father, King George VI, passed away. The coronation, following a period of mourning and meticulous preparation, marked the occasion. It was to be a day of celebration and solemnity, the centrepiece a ceremony that had remained largely the same for 1,000 years.

There have been 39 coronations held at Westminster Abbey since William the Conqueror’s in 1066, and the bones of the ceremony remain little changed in 1000 years. If you’d like to know more about the ceremony itself, I’d recommend Ian Lloyd’s brilliant The Throne: 1,000 Years of British Coronations, which is available on Amazon and details every little bit of history about this ultimate British display of pomp and circumstance.

Queen Elizabeth II’s Coronation

How old was the queen when she was crowned?

The Queen was 27 at the time. But she had been thinking about the day since she was 11. In May 1937, her father, King George VI, had asked her to record his coronation at Westminster Abbey so that she would appreciate it. “I thought it all very, very wonderful,” she wrote. “The arches and the beams at the top were covered in a sort of haze of wonder as Papa was crowned, or at least I thought so.” Then an admission. The service became “rather boring” towards the end because “it was all prayers”.

The young Queen was unlikely to feel bored when her turn came around, but rather a heady mix of excitement, pride and, perhaps, a little trepidation – not that she showed it. Lady Rosemary Spencer-Churchill, one of her Maids of Honour on the day, recalls her appearing “very confident”. Speaking on BBC Radio 4 in 2013, Lady Rosemary said: “She made us feel very confident too. The minute she arrived, everyone just felt everything was going to be all right.”

Preparations for the day begin

Hundreds of people camped on the pavements the night before in the cold and wet, so that they could catch a glimpse of their new Queen the next day. “The Queen’s subjects remained impressively British in their patient, companionable good nature,” wrote The New York Times. As her subjects waited for the Gold State Coach, wending its way from Buckingham Palace to the Abbey, they sang ‘Singing in the Rain’ and ‘It Ain’t Gonna Rain No More’ with hope and joy at a new Elizabethan era. The crowds would grow on Coronation Day to number three million.

The Abbey, the venue of every coronation since 1066, opened at 6 am to about 8,000 guests. Members of the Royal Family, peers and heads of state from across the world glided in after 8.30 am. Then, at 11.15, came the Queen with a procession of 250 people to verses from Psalm 22.

What did Queen Elizabeth II wear for her Coronation?

She wore a gown and a robe of white satin, made by Norman Hartnell, the fashion designer who had also designed her wedding dress. It was embroidered in gold and silver thread with Commonwealth motifs, from the English rose to Canadian maple leaves. The dress is on display as part of the new exhibition Queen Elizabeth II: Her Life in Style this year.

As glorious as the Queen looked, the gown and robes were so heavy that they got caught in the thick gold and blue carpet as she walked. “At one moment, I was going against the pile of the carpet, and I couldn’t move at all,” she said in a BBC documentary in 2018.

What was so special about Queen Elizabeth II’s Coronation?

The Coronation was the first to be televised – and the first event many saw on television. About 27 million people tuned in across the UK, with millions more around the world. About 11 million people tuned in on the radio.

What happens at the Coronation of a British monarch?

The three-hour service, conducted by the Archbishop of Canterbury, comprised six parts: the Recognition, the Oath, the Anointing, the Investiture (including the Crowning), the Enthronement and the Homage.

Every object was rich in significance. The Queen was crowned in St Edward’s Chair, made in 1300 for Edward I and used in every coronation since. During the ceremony, the Queen was handed the four symbols of authority: the Orb, the Sceptre, the Rod of Mercy and the Sovereign’s Ring. The Orb, a globe of gold surrounded by a cross with diamonds and emeralds, shone in the light. The Ring, also known as The Wedding Ring of England, glittered with sapphire and rubies.

At the moment of coronation, the Archbishop placed the St Edward’s Crown, one of the most iconic symbols of royalty, on the Queen’s head. Dating back to 1661, it is made of solid gold and encrusted with 440 precious and semi-precious stones. A cry of “God save the Queen” went up, and gun salutes were fired as crowds outside cheered.

What happened after Queen Elizabeth II’s Coronation ceremony?

The Queen held a coronation luncheon for 11 in the Abbey annex, where she and the Duke of Edinburgh ate smoked salmon, sausage rolls and chicken sandwiches. A banquet was held for guests, many from abroad, in the Great Hall of Westminster School, next door to the Abbey. The star dish was coronation chicken, a recipe created for the occasion.

For many people outside, the royal procession back from the Abbey to the Palace meant almost as much as the ceremony. At 7.2km, the route was designed to be seen by as many people as possible and took the participants, in their thousands, two hours. The Queen looked resplendent in her Purple Robe of Estate, embroidered with a cipher of the Queen and a border of olive branches and wheat ears, representing peace and plenty.

The Imperial State Crown

Now she wore the Imperial State Crown, a crown she had also worn at the end of the ceremony. It featured her favourite jewel, the Black Prince’s Ruby, worn by Henry V at the Battle of Agincourt. Both this crown, which the Queen later wore on State Openings of Parliament, and the St Edward’s Crown, were heavy, and despite her grace in the carriage, she endured a feat of strength. As she pointed out in the BBC documentary, “You can’t lean down to read your speech [wearing the crown]... Because if you did, your neck would break, and it would fall off.” To protect her posture, she practised wearing it beforehand. Lady Anne Glenconner, another Maid of Honour, recalled: “She used to wear it when she was writing letters. I think Prince Charles says he remembers going in and seeing her wearing it.”

In 2019, Lady Anne Glenconner published Lady in Waiting, which is available on Amazon. As a Maid of Honour at the Queen’s Coronation and former Lady in Waiting to Princess Margaret, this is a fascinating look into behind the scenes of royal life.

The Gold State Coach

On the day, the Queen had the added challenge of wearing the crown in a bumpy carriage - the Gold State Coach- a ride she described as “horrible”. She said, “It’s only sprung on leather, not very comfortable.” Just as she found the crown weighty, the horses had similar troubles with the four-ton coach. “We could only go at a walking pace,” said the Queen. “The horses couldn’t possibly go any faster.”

Observers cheered men from the Royal Navy, the Royal Air Force and the Army in the parade, as well as waving to figureheads from across the Commonwealth. But it was Queen Salote of Tonga who captured their hearts, refusing to put the roof up on her carriage, despite the British rain.

The evening celebrations

That evening, for the finale, the new Queen stood on the balcony of Buckingham Palace and turned on a show of lights along the Mall. She wore a silver evening gown, an ermine stole and a diamond diadem, and smiled at the thousands of people looking back at her. Beside her stood her husband, the Duke of Edinburgh, in a dinner jacket. At 10.30 pm, fireworks lit up the sky. “Portraits etched in lines of fire depicted the Queen, the Duke of Edinburgh and their two children, Prince Charles, Duke of Cornwall, and Princess Anne,” recorded The New York Times. “Each display brought a surge of cheering and applause.”

It was a fitting end to a remarkable day. Recalling her coronation, the Queen later said: “It’s the…beginning of one’s life, really, as a sovereign.” What a life it was.

Many people there on the day have managed to keep and preserve the Queen Elizabeth II Coronation Day book, made to commemorate the day in 1953, and there are copies available to buy on Amazon. As we mark 100 years since she was born, there have been several new biographies of Queen Elizabeth II in the last year as well. The world’s most knowledgeable royal biographer, Hugo Vickers, has just released Queen Elizabeth II, which is also available on Amazon, and is a must-read this anniversary year.
